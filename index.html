BLONDE HAIR

"The fact that blonde hair is some sort of signifier of desirability, despite the fact that

there isn’t really anything inherent that separates it from other hair colors, is

frankly a little weird. Getting into the origin of this idea seemingly ingrained in

westernized beauty ideals, it honestly seems even weirder. It all goes back to

ancient Greece, where blonde hair was considered very exotic. Aphrodite was

depicted as a blonde, and Sappho praised blonde hair in her poetry. Many

depictions of great Greek heroes show them as being blond, and prostitutes would

dye their hair blond using pigments such as saffron or plant ash. This influence

extended into Rome, and therefore into the territory it conquered. Even after the

fall of the empire itself, the Roman influence was very strong in Europe, and so hair

dyeing became the next hot trend during the medieval period. Fashionable ladies

would bleach their hair with dyeing agents at the time, such as sulfuric acid or even

horse urine (how very glamorous), and then spread it out over a specialized kind of

hat so it could be exposed to the sun. Scandinavians definitely had blonde-centric

beauty standards, considering it was much more genetically prevalent in the more

northerly areas. Western Europe had definitely declared that gentlemen, in fact, did prefer blondes."

THINNESS

“Thinness offers people privileges nobody speaks of because then we’d have to

admit we’ve all bought into it. That somehow we’ve all accepted that perfection

exists in a thin frame, in a skinny body. We connote thinness with a certain brand

of easy, carefree beauty, one that is reliably attainable without force or coercion.

It was the perfect example of how we’ve constantly been taught, through pop

culture, that women simply are thin, without cultivation. That chicness is inherent,

not calculated, or primed to perfection. Femme bodies are anthologized,

fetishized, and glorified, but they are never embraced with a totality of their

existence. Or the realities of their quirks, or clumsiness.” “Various studies have

shown that overweight people are seen as less conscientious, less agreeable,

less emotionally stable, less productive, lazy, lacking in self-discipline, and

even dishonest, sloppy, ugly, socially unattractive, and sexually unskilled; the

list goes on and on.* The stereotypes run so deep that even obese people hold

these same discriminatory beliefs about other obese people. Therefore, it may

come as no surprise that research has provided strong evidence that obese

people are paid less than their slimmer counterparts. Skinny women got paid

substantially higher salaries than heavier women, yet this relationship was

much less pronounced at the higher ends of the scale. Meaning that a female

employee weighing 50 kilograms would get paid substantially more than

someone weighing 60 kilograms, but the difference between 70 and 80 was

much less severe. That’s probably because – as Dan and Tim put it – “the

social preferences for a feminine body have already been violated”; you’re

either skinny or not, but once you’re over the (rather extreme) threshold, we

don’t care much anymore about the number on your scale.

Yet, this relationship looked very different for men. In contrast to the women

in the sample, men of moderate weight would get paid substantially more than

skinny men. But such a man of average weight would also get paid quite a bit

more than an obese person. Hence, being skinny for a woman would mean

more dosh, but for men it would mean less money – all in the order of

magnitude of $10,000-15,000 per year."
